Gödel, Escher, Bach and Contemporary Issues in Artificial Intelligence

Musa Haydar | May 27, 2026

A Greatly Edifying Book...

In the brief comments I want to make, I could not begin to unravel the Eternal Golden Braid which Douglas Hofstadter wove in his 1979 book, Gödel, Escher, Bach. It's a masterclass in the creativity and joy that can be found in multidisciplinary study, and though much of what he discusses in the book has certainly taken leaps since it's publication, this book lays an essential foundation to understanding contemporary issues in the age of "generative AI." But more than that, it inspires one to think about one's own thinking, to play with analogies that deepen one's understanding of their mind and the world they inhabits.

It's perhaps the first book I've read that I think everyone (literally, everyone) should read. It covers many challenging topics at an introductory level, encouraging readers to work through some problems left open to deepen their understanding. The chapters are interspersed with dialogues that playfully present the ideas discussed in the chapter, yet the playfulness (the wordplay, the wit) are suffused through the entire text. Then there are very important points made about AI which I will discuss shortly, and ultimately Hofstadter's thesis is about self-referential "Strange Loops" and the emergence of flexible, informal intelligence from rigid, formal systems. But to get to this point, Hofstadter takes us through the explanation of Gödel's Incompleteness Theorem, unsolved problems in microbiology and unsolvable problems in computability theory, analogies between mathematics, cognition, the paintings of Escher and Magritte, the music of Bach and Cage, and Zen Buddhism, to list a few. It's quite a long book after all, but it's delightful to read throughout. I really could not begin to expound what this book has either exposed me to afresh or (having a background in computer science) connected to things I already knew in enlightening ways.

...And Its Relevance to Contemporary Issues in AI

I will, however, write a bit focusing essentially on the final few chapters of the book, as they raise many important questions pertaining to contemporary (so called) AI systems. Since the emergence of LLMs, we are being bombarded with promises of what the future may hold in light of the present and prospective capabilities of AI systems. But this is met with severe controversy for a plethora of reasons: the environmental impact of training these systems, the economic and social implications of replacing technical and creative workers with AI, and the often poor-quality outputs (so called "hallucinations" or the creepy, lifeless quality of images) of the systems today.

Let's focus on this last point. It's interesting to think about the fallibility of LLMs in light of what Hofstadter seems to argue is an inevitability of intelligence: the possibility for error. For example, in discussing Alan Turing's "imitation game" (the Turing Test), Hofstadter gives the example of the time it takes a person to perform arithmetic and the potential for arithmetic mistakes as something characteristic of human intelligence (p. 573). He later says (in his 10 Questions and Speculations) that if AI systems are truly intelligent in the way humans are, they would be subject to the same errors as their "minds" work by the complex interaction and manipulation of symbols rather than on the level of formal hardware performing the calculations. Indeed, LLMs and other AI systems today operate via intractable "neural networks," statistical models which emulate the interactions of neurons, and upon which patterns seem to emerge and relate with each other. Does this mean we have therefore achieved artificial intelligence?

I think the most striking shortcoming of LLM systems is their lack of any notion of truth. The question of how truth might be encoded in AI systems was explored in the chapter "Artificial Intelligence: Retrospects," and it is often a missing yet key element in understanding the limitations of LLMs. LLMs only encode "knowledge" as what is ultimately statistical probability. That is, LLMs have no beliefs according to which they can evaluate whether a statement prompted or outputted is "true". Instead, LLMs often say things that seem "true" because, statistically, the most likely utterance in many cases is a true one. (Hence also the repetition of clichés and the frequent occurrence of sycophancy.) But LLMs are disturbingly often wrong.

We seem to have converged on calling these errors "hallucinations," but the problem with such terminology is that it implies a dichotomy between when LLMs are "correct" and when they are "hallucinating." But this does not create an accurate picture of things, as there is no fundamental difference between when an LLM is correct or not, and most LLM outputs appear somewhere along the spectrum of incorrectness rather than being either correct or not. Often this incorrectness is hidden in jargon and phrases which seem correct. But because of the lack beliefs or models of truth, LLMs are always "bullshitting," in the sense put forth by Frankfurt (and detailed in a paper published two years ago, Hicks et al., "ChatGPT Is Bullshit"): LLMs "speak" with no consideration for the truth (in fact, with no considerations altogether). Rather, they simply produce text that replicates human text.

Along these lines, compare the humorous, nonsensical output of Hofstadter's sentence generation program (p. 620) with the Shakespeare generation Recurrent Neural Network (RNN) from 2015, also nonsense. Compare again with present-day LLMs which are known to output clichés and produce sentences in particular constructions and styles which give them away as LLM-generated. Though these sentences seem meaningful, the paper linked above ("ChatGPT Is Bullshit") raises questions about their meaningfulness in the first place. Regardless, I think an LLM would fail a Turing Test administered by someone privy to the manner of their output. Perhaps with LLMs, we are experiencing en-masse what Weizenbaum noted about the "Doctor" and "Parry" programs: most people "can explain the computer's intellectual feats only by bringing to bear the single analogy available to them, that is, their model of their own capacity to think" (quoted on p. 600). This is, of course, a very skeptical position, but given the issues listed above, to say nothing of the corporate hysteria and people's deeply growing dependence on these faulty systems, skepticism is important.

Another point of relevance brought forth was the Bongard Problems, which Hofstadter spends a bit of time thinking about what it would take for an AI system to be able to reason through. These problems involve reasoning on various levels and are still a significant benchmark. LLMs today are presently able to simulate reasoning by breaking down problems, such that their output for smaller problems informs their work towards the solution. This is a fascinating capability, and some argue that this process is reasoning per se, as human reasoning often occurs as just such an internal monologue. However, this reasoning still occurs without consideration for truth, and Bongard problems pose a challenge for LLM reasoning systems (see Malkinski et al. 2024 and Wüst et al. 2025 as examples).

There are many other important lines of thinking brought up in the final few chapters of Gödel, Escher, Bach. One is the issue of originality and authorship in artifacts produce with or even entirely by AI systems. Perhaps generative AI systems now, with their intractable neural networks, meet Hofstadter's criteria that "the internal structure of such a program is based on something similar to the 'symbols' in our brains and their triggering patterns" to consider a piece of AI-generated music to be "composed by a computer" (p. 609). Nevertheless, there is a remarkable distaste for generated images and music. Perhaps this is due to the lack of a sense of beauty or emotional state in these systems (some more problems brought up in the book). My paper argues that this distaste occurs due to the lack of creative intent expressed in generated images, which is the basis upon which representational art is evaluated. Even if AI systems now are capable of "composing music" and creating images, without intentionality, a sense of beauty and taste, and an experienced emotional palette, these works seem to fall short of really being art.

Hofstadter's 10 Questions and Speculations about AI are also a fascinating artifact with which we might direct some reflections on the capabilities, promises, and perils of the kind of AI we have today, though I'm not going to pursue these questions further. Certainly we are far closer to it than we were almost 50 years (and than we were 5 years ago). But I think what Hofstadter coined "Tesler's Theorem" holds true: "AI is whatever has not been done yet."

Historically, people have been naïve about what qualities, if mechanized, would undeniably constitute intelligence. Sometimes it seems as though each new step towards AI, rather than producing something which everyone agrees is real intelligence, merely reveals what real intelligence is not. If intelligence involves learning, creativity, emotional responses, a sense of beauty, a sense of self, then there is a long road ahead [...] (p. 573)
